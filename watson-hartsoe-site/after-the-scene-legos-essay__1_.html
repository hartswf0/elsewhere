After the Scene

LEGOS and the problem of keeping a world after artificial intelligence has written the next one

Watson Hartsoe
Design research · 2026
Abstract

A generative model can write a confession, burn down a house, or split a city into rival futures. What it cannot be trusted to do is keep those changes intact. The house returns. The confession loses force. One branch leaks into another. LEGOS approaches this failure by moving narrative state outside prose. People, places, desires, blocks, changes, and resolutions become explicit parts of a world that can be rearranged, traced, forked, and restored. The work is still experimental, but its central wager is clear: the future of AI narrative depends less on producing better sentences than on preserving what those sentences make true.

The first scene is easy

The room is cold. A woman sits across from a machine that knows the name of her dead brother. The machine tells her something no one else could know. She believes it.

This is where generative artificial intelligence is strongest. It can summon a room, a voice, a secret, and the exact pressure required to make the secret matter. The scene arrives whole enough to feel inevitable.

Then the story continues.

Twenty minutes later, the machine forgets the brother’s name. An hour later, the woman trusts it for reasons unrelated to what happened. In another branch, the brother was never mentioned, but the machine behaves as though the confession still occurred. The sentences remain fluent. The world has come apart.

We tend to call this a memory problem. That makes the failure sound smaller than it is. The machine may retrieve the earlier conversation perfectly and still fail to know what remains true. A passage can say that the door was locked, that the key was destroyed, and that the woman chose not to leave. The next scene must still decide which of those facts are active, which have changed, and which belong to a different history.

The danger is not that the machine forgets a detail. It is that nothing in the system is responsible for keeping the world intact.

A story needs somewhere to live after the sentence ends

LEGOS begins with a plain move: it takes the things a story depends on and gives them somewhere to exist outside the prose.

A place. A person or object. Something wanted. Something in the way. A change. A resolution. These are not presented as an explanation of every story ever told. They are a working set of handles. The system can point to them, place them, connect them, move them, and remember when their relation changes.

This matters because prose is good at implication. State requires commitment.

When a city is described as a home, a prison, and a destination in three different scenes, a reader can understand the shift. A machine needs to know whether the city itself changed, whether the character’s relation to it changed, or whether the story has contradicted itself. LEGOS makes that question visible by separating the thing from the role it plays.

The thing

The city, the woman, the key, the promise, the machine.

What it is doing now

Holding, blocking, drawing, changing, or resolving something in this part of the story.

The distinction is simple, but it opens a difficult possibility. The same city can be a setting in one moment, an obstacle in the next, and the object of return years later. Its identity survives. Its place in the story changes.

The rules are where the argument becomes real

In one of the repository’s grid experiments, places can give rise to actors. Actors move toward what they want. Wants create opposition. Contact with opposition produces change. Resolutions do not end the world; they settle into the ground from which another cycle begins.

Those rules are not profound because they are true. They are profound because they are exposed.

Most stories hide their governing beliefs inside style. A hero needs a goal. Desire produces conflict. Resolution restores order. We rarely see these assumptions as rules because they arrive disguised as plot. LEGOS turns them into procedures that can be altered and watched.

What happens if a solution creates a debt instead of a new goal? What happens if a place produces memory rather than a new actor? What happens if an obstacle becomes a home?

The point is not to automate a universal story. It is to make the world’s assumptions available for revision.

What changes

The author stops controlling every sentence and begins controlling the conditions under which sentences can remain consequential.

A branch should remember why it exists

Most branching systems are graveyards. The player chooses left; the right-hand path disappears. The author may keep the tree, but the running world keeps only the selected path.

LEGOS treats a branch as a history with a parent, a point of departure, and a state of its own. A snapshot can preserve the world before the split. A later branch can be restored without pretending that the intervening events never happened elsewhere.

This changes the meaning of memory. A woman who refused an offer is not the same as a woman who never received it. A city rebuilt after catastrophe is not the same as a city that was never destroyed. A machine forgiven in one branch should not inherit forgiveness in a branch where it never confessed.

The mainline matters only because the system remembers the lives it did not choose.

The repository does not yet solve every problem this creates. It preserves branches more readily than it compares or merges them. But the direction is decisive: a story’s unrealized histories belong to the world, not just to the author’s notes.

The real author is whoever sets the conditions

There is a temptation to describe a system like this as liberation: the story escapes the author and begins to live on its own.

That is only half true.

The person who stops choosing every sentence begins choosing the rules beneath the sentences. Who gets to want? What counts as a block? When does a change become a resolution? Which history becomes the mainline? These decisions govern the world more deeply than dialogue.

LEGOS is valuable because it can make that power visible. A rule can be inspected. A transformation can be traced. A branch can be reopened. A default can be replaced. What once passed as the natural shape of the story becomes an authored condition.

This is where the project becomes more than a technique for keeping continuity. It becomes a way to expose the hidden government of a generated world.

The stakes

The question is not whether an AI can invent a compelling next scene. The question is whether readers, writers, and players can see and contest the conditions that make one future possible and another impossible.

One world, not a shelf of experiments

The repository already contains the pieces: named things and their relations; grids that allow proximity and time to matter; histories that split without disappearing; ways to translate narrative meaning into the language of planning systems.

The next step is not another interface. It is one shared world beneath all of them.

The same woman, city, key, promise, and machine should be editable in one view, advanced in another, forked in a third, and rendered by any language model without losing identity or history. A change made anywhere should become visible everywhere. A scene should propose a change to the world, not quietly rewrite it.

That would make LEGOS more than a vocabulary. It would make it an accountable place for a story to live.

Conclusion

Artificial intelligence has made the next sentence abundant. Consequence remains scarce.

LEGOS matters because it begins after the impressive scene, at the moment when the story must keep what just happened. It gives people, places, desires, barriers, changes, and resolutions a life outside prose. It preserves the path into the present. It makes the rules beneath the world available for inspection.

The future of AI narrative will not be decided by the model that writes the most beautiful room. It will be decided by the system that remembers who entered it, what they changed, and why the door can no longer lead to the same place.
